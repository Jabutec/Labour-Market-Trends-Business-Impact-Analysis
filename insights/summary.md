# South African Labour Market Analysis — Key Insights

**Data Source:** Statistics South Africa — Quarterly Labour Force Survey (QLFS)  
**Period Covered:** Oct-Dec 2023 to Oct-Dec 2024  
**Tools Used:** Python (Pandas, Matplotlib), SQL (SQLite), Power BI

---

## 1. Underemployment is Growing — And Men Are Catching Up Fast

Total underemployment rose from **678,000 (Oct-Dec 2023)** to **788,000 (Oct-Dec 2024)** — a **16.3% year-on-year increase**.

| Gender | Oct-Dec 2023 | Oct-Dec 2024 | Change |
| ------ | ------------ | ------------ | ------ |
| Women  | 386,000      | 409,000      | +6.0%  |
| Men    | 292,000      | 379,000      | +29.9% |

**Business Implication:** The sharp rise in male underemployment (+29.9%) suggests growing pressure in male-dominated sectors like construction, manufacturing, and transport. Businesses in these sectors may face a large pool of available workers willing to take on more hours — a signal for flexible hiring strategies.

---

## 2. Community Services and Private Households Are the Hardest Hit Industries

| Industry                    | Underemployed (Oct-Dec 2024) |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Community & Social Services | 210,000                      |
| Private Households          | 200,000                      |
| Trade                       | 104,000                      |
| Construction                | 91,000                       |
| Finance                     | 89,000                       |

**Business Implication:** High underemployment in community services and private households reflects low-wage, part-time work structures in these sectors. Businesses and policymakers should consider formalising employment in these sectors to improve worker stability and productivity.

---

## 3. Elementary and Domestic Workers Are the Most Underemployed Occupations

| Occupation            | Underemployed (Oct-Dec 2024) |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Elementary            | 324,000                      |
| Domestic Worker       | 139,000                      |
| Craft & Related Trade | 115,000                      |
| Sales & Services      | 87,000                       |

**Business Implication:** Underemployment is heavily concentrated in low-skill, low-wage occupations. This points to a structural mismatch — the economy is not generating enough full-time, skilled work. Upskilling and reskilling programmes targeting these groups could reduce underemployment over time.

---

## 4. South Africa Has an Overwork Problem Too

While underemployment is a concern, overwork is an even bigger issue:

| Hours Worked Per Week  | Number of Workers |
| ---------------------- | ----------------- |
| Less than 15 hours     | 509,000           |
| 15–29 hours            | 1,256,000         |
| 30–39 hours            | 1,230,000         |
| 40–45 hours (standard) | 9,554,000         |
| More than 45 hours     | 4,529,000         |

**4.5 million workers** are working more than 45 hours per week — nearly **9x more** than those working under 15 hours.

**Business Implication:** Overwork is a significant workforce wellbeing risk. Businesses relying heavily on long working hours face risks of burnout, reduced productivity, and higher turnover. This data supports the case for better workforce planning and workload distribution.

---

## 5. Self-Employment Is on the Rise

Own-account (self-employed) workers grew from **1,687,000 to 1,932,000** — a **14.5% increase** year-on-year.

| Gender | Oct-Dec 2023 | Oct-Dec 2024 | Change |
| ------ | ------------ | ------------ | ------ |
| Both   | 1,687,000    | 1,932,000    | +14.5% |
| Women  | 725,000      | 824,000      | +13.7% |
| Men    | 962,000      | 1,108,000    | +15.1% |

**Business Implication:** The rise in self-employment signals a growing gig economy in South Africa. Businesses should consider how to engage freelancers and contractors effectively, while policymakers should look at social protection gaps for self-employed workers.

---

## 6. The Workforce Is Predominantly Employee-Based

| Employment Status       | Oct-Dec 2024       |
| ----------------------- | ------------------ |
| Employee                | 14,087,000 (82.5%) |
| Own-account Worker      | 1,932,000 (11.3%)  |
| Employer                | 966,000 (5.7%)     |
| Unpaid Household Member | 92,000 (0.5%)      |

**Business Implication:** The dominance of formal employment (82.5%) means traditional labour market policies and employer-employee relationships remain the primary lever for workforce improvement in South Africa.

---

## Summary — Key Takeaways for Business

1. **Underemployment is rising fastest among men** — flexible hiring opportunities exist in male-dominated sectors
2. **Low-skill occupations are most affected** — reskilling investment is needed at scale
3. **Overwork is a bigger problem than underwork** — workforce wellbeing policies are critical
4. **Self-employment is growing** — the gig economy needs better support structures
5. **Community services and private households** need formalisation to improve worker stability
